Knowledge and Context Management in the Age of AI

Your AI is only as good as the knowledge behind it. Learn to represent it, connect it, extract it, query it, discover from it, and keep it good.

The course has no prerequisites except for:

  • Curiosity
  • Something you actually want your organization to know
  • Patience for the unglamorous truth that good AI starts with good knowledge

The course is "hands-on" but no heavy coding is required: you will get to play with small examples to feel what a representation, a graph, or an extraction buys you — and where each one breaks.

Who this course is for
  • Engineers and engineering managers building with AI: RAG, agents, and assistants are only as good as the knowledge they sit on. Learn to build that knowledge layer deliberately.
  • Product managers and knowledge owners: Turn a pile of documents into a measurable, improvable knowledge asset — and know when it is good enough to ship.
  • Data and platform teams: Choose representations, model a knowledge graph, design extraction pipelines, and decide what to query vs. what to discover.
  • Executives (compact course): Understand why knowledge quality is now a product-quality lever, and what to ask the teams who own it.

How the course works

Every lesson is built the same way:

  • It has tiles describing what we cover. You can see if you already know the content, need a refresher, or it is new for you.
  • It takes one stage of the knowledge lifecycle — represent, connect, extract, query, discover, improve — so you meet it on its own before it combines with the others.
  • It keeps quality of knowledge as the through-line: every stage is judged by whether the knowledge it produces is correct, complete, consistent, fresh, and findable.
  • It contrasts good practice against the trap that fools people — shown through a worked example.
  • It hands you a hands-on example you can poke at, plus an optional code path for those who want to go deeper.

The lifecycle is a loop, not a line: Lesson 7 feeds the quality signals from Lesson 1 — and the gaps surfaced by querying (L5) and discovery (L6) — back into representation, the graph, and extraction.

What you will learn

By the end of the course, "knowledge" will look like a managed, measurable asset — not a folder of documents you hope someone reads.

You will be able to:

  • Define what quality of knowledge means for your domain, and measure it.
  • Choose how to represent knowledge — text, schemas, taxonomies, embeddings, graphs — for the job at hand.
  • Model a knowledge graph and decide when it beats a plain vector store.
  • Extract structured knowledge from messy sources, with LLMs, and know how much to trust it.
  • Query knowledge well — keyword, semantic, structured, RAG, and GraphRAG — and tell grounded answers from guesses.
  • Discover what you didn't know to ask: importance, communities, and missing links via graph methods.
  • Run an improvement loop that detects gaps, staleness, and contradictions and closes them.

What the course is NOT about
  • We are not training foundation models — we manage the knowledge that AI systems consume and produce.
  • We are not doing organizational change management or "KM as HR" — the focus is the technical knowledge layer behind AI products.
  • We are not building a full production RAG stack — we build the knowledge it depends on.

The lessons

Seven 1-hour sessions following the knowledge lifecycle: represent → connect → extract → query → discover → improve, with quality of knowledge as the through-line. Each isolates one stage and ties it back to knowledge quality. Every session comes with optional hands-on examples for those who want to go further.

L1
Quality of Knowledge

What "knowledge" is, what makes it good, and how to measure it — the scorecard the rest of the course optimizes.

Content
  • Data → information → knowledge: a useful distinction, and where the tidy DIKW pyramid lies to you
  • The dimensions of knowledge quality: correctness, completeness/coverage, consistency, freshness, relevance, provenance, findability, actionability
  • How to measure each dimension — gold questions, answerability, citation correctness, contradiction rate, staleness
  • Why in the age of AI the quality of the knowledge base sets the ceiling on the quality of every answer the AI gives (garbage in, garbage out)
  • Building a knowledge-quality scorecard you can actually track
Skills you unlock
  • Name the quality dimensions that matter for your domain and weight them
  • Turn "is our knowledge good?" into a measurable scorecard
  • Connect a drop in AI answer quality to the knowledge defect behind it
  • Set a "good enough to ship" bar for a knowledge base
Quality dimension in focus

All of them — at once. This lesson lays out the full scorecard. Every later lesson improves one or two of these dimensions; here you learn what they are and how to measure them.

L2
Knowledge Representation

The forms knowledge can take — text, schemas, taxonomies, triples, embeddings — and how the choice decides what you can later do with it.

Content
  • The representation spectrum: unstructured text, semi-structured, structured (tables, taxonomies, ontologies, triples), and vector/embedding representations
  • Symbolic vs. sub-symbolic knowledge — what each makes easy and what each makes impossible
  • Taxonomies, ontologies, and schemas: the vocabulary that makes knowledge interoperable
  • Chunking and embeddings for RAG — what gets captured, what gets lost
  • Hybrid representations, and choosing one for a concrete use case
Skills you unlock
  • Pick a representation that fits the questions you need to answer
  • Sketch a taxonomy or lightweight schema for a domain
  • Reason about what an embedding representation can and cannot retrieve
  • Combine representations instead of forcing everything into one
Quality dimension in focus

Findability & consistency. The representation decides what can be retrieved, compared, and kept non-contradictory. A bad representation caps quality before a single answer is generated.

L3
Knowledge Graphs

Connecting facts into a graph of entities and relations — the structure we'll populate, query, and mine. What a KG is, and when it beats a vector store.

Content
  • Anatomy of a knowledge graph: entities, relations, properties, and the schema/ontology on top
  • RDF triples vs. labeled property graphs; a first look at SPARQL and Cypher
  • Why connection is its own representation decision — the graph is the structure the next lessons depend on
  • KG vs. vector store — relational/multi-hop questions vs. "find similar"
  • When a knowledge graph is worth the cost — and when it is not
Skills you unlock
  • Model a domain as entities and relations
  • Read and write basic graph queries (SPARQL / Cypher)
  • Decide between a graph, a vector store, or both for a use case
  • Design the schema that extraction (L4) will populate
Quality dimension in focus

Completeness & consistency. A graph makes coverage gaps visible as missing edges and contradictions as conflicting triples — defects that stay hidden in a pile of documents.

L4
Knowledge Extraction

Filling the graph. Turning messy sources into the entities, relations, and facts the schema asked for — and knowing how much to trust the result.

Content
  • From documents to structure: entity, relation, and event extraction — filling the L3 schema
  • Classic IE (NER, rules, patterns) vs. LLM-based extraction with structured outputs
  • Schema-guided extraction and function calling — making an LLM emit exactly the nodes and edges the graph expects
  • Entity resolution, deduplication, and normalization — the same thing said five ways
  • Extraction quality: precision/recall, hallucination risk, and keeping provenance
Skills you unlock
  • Design an extraction pipeline from source to graph-ready records
  • Use an LLM to extract to the L3 schema, and constrain it
  • Resolve and merge entities that refer to the same thing
  • Evaluate extraction quality and trace every fact back to its source
Quality dimension in focus

Correctness & provenance. Extraction is where errors and hallucinations enter the knowledge base — and where you either capture the source of every fact or lose the ability to trust it later.

L5
Knowledge Querying

Getting out what you already know. Search, semantic retrieval, structured queries, RAG, and GraphRAG — when you know what you're looking for.

Content
  • The two modes of using knowledge: querying (retrieve what's known) vs. discovery (find what's unknown, L6)
  • Keyword vs. semantic (embedding) retrieval — exact match vs. "find similar"
  • Structured queries over the graph — SPARQL/Cypher, traversal, paths, multi-hop questions
  • RAG and GraphRAG — grounding an LLM's answer in retrieved knowledge, with citations
  • Combining retrieval modes, and reading what a query can and can't guarantee
Skills you unlock
  • Pick the retrieval mode that fits the question — keyword, semantic, structured, or traversal
  • Write graph queries that answer multi-hop and relational questions
  • Build a RAG/GraphRAG answer that cites its sources (using L4 provenance)
  • Diagnose a bad answer as a retrieval failure, not a model failure
Quality dimension in focus

Findability & relevance. Querying is where findability is cashed in: the right mode retrieves the right knowledge, and citations make relevance checkable. A bad answer here is usually a retrieval failure, not a model one.

L6
Knowledge Discovery

Finding what you didn't know to ask for. Graph methods — centrality, communities, link prediction — turn the knowledge base into new, latent knowledge.

Content
  • Discovery vs. querying (L5): surfacing latent knowledge vs. retrieving known knowledge
  • Centrality — what's authoritative, central, or a single point of failure
  • Community detection — clustering related knowledge, finding topics and silos
  • Link prediction — suggesting relations that should exist but are missing (the gap radar)
  • Node embeddings and GNNs, conceptually — when to reach for them (and when not)
Skills you unlock
  • Match a discovery task — gap detection, dedup, topic/silo finding, expertise location — to the right method
  • Interpret centrality and community results without over-reading them
  • Use link prediction to surface missing knowledge before users hit the gap
  • Turn a discovery output into a prioritized, reviewable action
Quality dimension in focus

Completeness & relevance. Discovery reveals what's missing or disconnected (link prediction, silos) and what matters most (centrality) — turning a static graph into a tool that finds its own gaps.

L7
Knowledge Improvement

Knowledge is a living asset that decays. The loop that detects gaps, staleness, and contradictions — and closes them with humans and AI.

Content
  • Knowledge decays: it goes stale, develops gaps, and accumulates contradictions
  • The improvement loop: detect → prioritize → fix → verify, scored against the L1 scorecard
  • Feedback signals: usage, failed queries (L5), discovery findings (L6), user feedback, AI failures
  • Using AI to improve KM: detecting contradictions, proposing updates, filling gaps, summarizing
  • Governance: human-in-the-loop review, provenance (L4), and not letting the AI quietly rewrite the truth
Skills you unlock
  • Design a knowledge-improvement loop with explicit detect/fix/verify stages
  • Use querying and discovery signals to prioritize what to fix first
  • Combine human review with AI assistance safely
  • Close the loop: feed quality metrics back to representation, the graph, and extraction
Quality dimension in focus

Freshness & the whole scorecard, over time. The lifecycle becomes a loop: this lesson keeps every dimension from L1 from quietly degrading as the world moves.
